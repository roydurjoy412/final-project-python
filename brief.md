Final Project Instructions
Data Analysis Project with Pandas, NumPy, and Matplotlib
Total Marks: 40
Choose a real dataset, answer meaningful questions, justify your data decisions, and present
evidence-based findings.
Project goal: carry out a mini data investigation using Pandas, NumPy, and Matplotlib. Your work must show data
understanding, justified cleaning, feature engineering, analytical depth, clear visual communication, and honest
reflection on limitations.
1. Project Overview
This project is designed to test not only whether you can use Python libraries, but whether you can think like an
analyst. A strong submission will define clear questions, prepare the data carefully, perform meaningful analysis,
and defend its conclusions with evidence.
• This is not a chart-making task. Random plots without a purpose will not score highly.
• You must answer 2 to 3 meaningful questions using data, not just describe the dataset.
• Your final work should demonstrate technical skill, reasoning, interpretation, and professionalism.
2. Required Tools
• Pandas: for loading, inspecting, cleaning, filtering, grouping, aggregating, transforming, and summarizing the dataset.
• NumPy: for meaningful numerical work such as custom calculations, array-based processing, percentiles, z-scores,
normalization, or other statistical computation.
• Matplotlib: for visualizations that directly support your questions and findings.
• You may work in Google Colab, Jupyter Notebook, or a local Python environment.
• You may use small supporting libraries if necessary, but the core analysis must clearly depend on Pandas, NumPy,
and Matplotlib.
• It is not acceptable to import one of the required libraries without using it meaningfully.
3. Dataset Requirements
• Your dataset must be real and obtained from a valid source such as Kaggle, UCI Machine Learning Repository,
data.gov, the World Bank, WHO, UNESCO, or another credible open-data platform.
• It must contain at least 1000 rows and at least 6 columns.
• It must be rich enough to support comparison, interpretation, and multiple forms of analysis.
• You must mention the dataset source clearly in both your code/notebook and your report.
• Do not choose a dataset that is tiny, trivial, mostly empty, or unsuitable for meaningful analysis.
• Strong preference: choose a dataset that includes mixed data types, missing values, date/time information,
categorical and numerical variables, or enough complexity to make your analysis interesting.
Examples of suitable themes: student performance, sales, finance, transport, health, sports, environment, public
policy, business operations, or survey data.
4. What Your Project Must Include
4.1 Problem Definition
• State what dataset you selected.
• Explain what real topic or issue the dataset represents.
• Write 2 to 3 meaningful analytical questions that your project will answer.
• Briefly explain why those questions matter.
4.2 Data Understanding
• Show the dataset shape, column names, data types, sample rows, summary statistics, and missing-value summary.
• Do not only print outputs. Briefly explain what those outputs tell you about the structure and quality of the data.
4.3 Data Cleaning and Preparation
• Perform at least 3 meaningful cleaning or preprocessing steps.
Page 1• Examples include handling missing values, removing duplicates, converting data types, fixing inconsistent entries,
formatting dates, renaming columns, and removing or analyzing outliers.
• For every major cleaning step, write a short justification: what the problem was, what you did, and why that decision
was reasonable.
4.4 Feature Engineering
• Create at least 2 new derived columns from existing data.
• Examples include age groups, month or year from a date column, family size, profit margin, normalized values, fare
category, rolling average, or a custom score.
• You are not allowed to rely only on the original columns. Your new features should help support later analysis.
4.5 Data Analysis
• Perform at least 6 meaningful analysis operations.
• Your project must include at least 2 subgroup comparisons.
• It must include at least 1 relationship analysis between variables.
• It must include at least 1 outlier, anomaly, or unusual-pattern analysis.
• It must include at least 1 custom NumPy-based computation.
• Examples of strong analysis include filtering, grouping and aggregation, ranking, trend analysis, distribution analysis,
correlation-style comparison, frequency analysis, and custom metrics.
4.6 Visualization
• Create at least 4 meaningful charts using Matplotlib.
• Every chart must have a clear title, labeled axes where appropriate, and readable formatting.
• For each chart, write 2 to 3 sentences explaining what it shows, why it matters, and what insight can be taken from
it.
• Accepted chart types include line charts, bar charts, histograms, scatter plots, box plots, pie charts where
appropriate, heatmaps built with Matplotlib, and multi-panel figures.
4.7 Findings and Discussion
• Write at least 5 clear, evidence-based insights from your analysis.
• Avoid weak statements such as “the graph looks good” or “the data is interesting.”
• Strong findings should connect your evidence back to the questions you originally asked.
4.8 Limitations
• Include a short section on limitations.
• Discuss issues such as missing values, sample bias, incomplete variables, preprocessing choices, or the fact that
correlation does not prove causation.
5. Code Submission Instructions
• Your project code must be submitted through either a GitHub repository link or a Google Colab notebook link.
• You must upload the code link to the Google Sheet provided by the instructor.
• Your code should be clearly organized into sections such as: import libraries, load data, inspect data, clean data,
engineer features, analyze, visualize, and summarize findings.
• Your notebook or repository must clearly include your name, student ID, project title, and dataset source.
• Your code/notebook must run correctly. Broken links, inaccessible repositories, or missing files may lead to mark
deduction.
• If you use GitHub, make sure the repository is public or accessible to the instructor.
• If you use Google Colab, make sure the sharing permission allows the instructor to view the notebook.

• Your work must be your own. Copying notebooks, repositories, or reports from the internet or from classmates is not
acceptable.
